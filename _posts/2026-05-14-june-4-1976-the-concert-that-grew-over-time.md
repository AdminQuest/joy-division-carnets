On June 4, 1976, the Sex Pistols play the Lesser Free Trade Hall in Manchester. As a fact, it is modest: one hall, a small audience, a London band not yet fixed in its later canon. Nothing in the immediate scale of the event fully explains the almost sacred weight it later acquires in Manchester’s cultural memory.

Yet this is where the story begins to tighten.

In his retrospective testimony, Peter Hook treats the evening as a scene of revelation. What he remembers first is not virtuosity, nor even a stable musical form. He sees something else: musicians who seem to belong to the same social world as him, and who suddenly make practice possible. Punk operates here as permission. It does not yet provide a method; it removes a prohibition.

The distinction matters. This concert does not found Joy Division as a cause mechanically produces its effect. Rather, it authorises a passage into action. Hook and Bernard Sumner do not leave the hall with the sound of « Transmission » in mind, nor with the cold architecture of *Unknown Pleasures*. They leave with a rougher intuition: one can make music without waiting to be granted the right to do so.

The myth then expands. Around the Lesser Free Trade Hall, an entire scene gradually gathers: future Buzzcocks, future Smiths, future Fall, future Factory figures, witnesses, critics, photographers, producers. The list of those present becomes almost as important as the concert itself. It draws a retrospective map of Manchester, as if the city had briefly fitted inside a single room.

This is precisely where caution is required. The sources converge on the importance of the concert, but they also point to its memorial inflation. The second Sex Pistols concert in Manchester, on July 20, 1976, plays a more direct role for Ian Curtis, who misses the first one. The Electric Circus later becomes a more durable laboratory for the local scene. June 4 is therefore not the single origin. It is a primitive scene, in the critical sense: less an absolute beginning than a point where the later story condenses.

The Lesser Free Trade Hall does not create Joy Division. It opens a breach. Into that breach rush amateurism, anger, the desire for form, and the new certainty that one no longer needs to know how to play in order to begin saying something.
