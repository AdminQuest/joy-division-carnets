## The Sex Pistols' concert at the Lesser Free Trade Hall on 4 June 1976 is, in the historiography of British music, a singularly paradoxical event: a confidential evening that no one scarcely notices at the time, and which retrospectively transforms itself into a founding scene of a cultural revolution. To understand this paradox is to understand something fundamental about the way in which the Joy Division myth constructs itself — and about the intellectual caution that the analysis of these Mancunian years demands.

## A Modest Evening, a Half-Empty Room

The facts, first. The Lesser Free Trade Hall is an annex of Manchester's Free Trade Hall, a venue of modest dimensions, nestled in Peter Street in the heart of the city centre. On 4 June 1976, two local students — Peter McNeish and Howard Trafford, soon to become Pete Shelley and Howard Devoto of the Buzzcocks — organise the event with derisory means. Tickets are sold for fifty pence, advertised discreetly in the Manchester Evening News. The attendance does not exceed forty people¹.

Forty people. The figure deserves to be retained, before memorial inflation dissolves it into legend.

Within this handful of spectators are, however, Bernard Sumner and Peter Hook, soon to be founders of Warsaw and then Joy Division. The photographer Kevin Cummins is present. Steven Patrick Morrissey, future singer of The Smiths, attends the evening. Mark E. Smith, who will found The Fall, is there. Martin Hannett, future producer of Joy Division, likewise. The journalist Paul Morley, who will become one of the most influential chroniclers of British music, is in the room². This convergence is real. It is not invented. What is invented, however, is the idea that this concert causes anything.

For the performance lasts approximately thirty minutes. The sound reaches an indescribable saturation. The Sex Pistols play songs that will become punk classics. Sumner, Hook and their contemporaries leave galvanised. But the retrospective accounts of this galvanisation — recorded years later, often on the occasion of anniversaries, commemorations or publications — all display the characteristic features of what is called constructed memory: details sharpen, emotions become more precise, the significance of the evening grows as the history of the group is written³.

## The Mechanisms of Memorial Inflation

Chris Ott, in his work devoted to Unknown Pleasures, is one of the rare critics to name the phenomenon explicitly. He speaks of "memorial inflation" to qualify the way in which the number of people claiming to have attended the concert of 4 June 1976 never ceases to grow over the years, far exceeding the capacity of the room. David Nolan, in I Swear IWas There (2006), documents this mythological expansion with precise irony: the very title of the work is a citation from testimonies gathered, and constitutes an implicit admission about the nature of the phenomenon⁴.

This mechanism is not peculiar to Manchester. Every emerging musical scene produces its founding myths. What distinguishes the concert of 4 June 1976 is the extraordinary density of witnesses who, having become major cultural figures, subsequently had the opportunity to recount publicly their presence in that room. Each account amplifies the preceding ones. The modesty of the event disappears beneath the weight of the narratives that surround it.

One must therefore carefully distinguish two things: on one side, the objective event — a confidential punk evening in a small Manchester hall, organised by two Sex Pistols fans; on the other, the meaning retrospectively accorded to that event by actors who had time to measure its scope, and who sometimes had an interest in amplifying it.

Peter Hook, in his memoirs Unknown Pleasures: Inside Joy Division, recounts that the very next day after the concert, he rushes to Mamelocks, the local instrument shop, to buy his first bass guitar, choosing the cheapest model because he likes its colour⁵. A delightful anecdote, frequently cited. It may well be true. It also has the perfect form of a founding narrative: an immediate gesture, almost naïve, triggered by a revelation. These musicians' accounts offer precious material for the historian; they do not replace documentary evidence.

## The Concert, One Catalyst Among Others

It would be reductive, however, to approach 4 June 1976 solely through the prism of its mythologisation. The event is real. Its impact on the Mancunian scene is documented by sources other than the sole recollections of participants. The relationship between the restricted audience of the first concert and the long list of musicians, journalists and producers who were present constitutes, in itself, a sociological fact: this evening gathers, in exceptional concentration, a part of the actors who will manufacture the Manchester post-punk scene in the years that follow⁶.

Daniele De Luca, in his analysis of the sociopolitical context of 1970s England as applied to Joy Division, qualifies this concert as a "moment of collective crystallisation"⁷. The formulation is apt, on condition that one does not read it teleologically: the concert does not create the scene, it reveals it to itself. What has been fermenting for months in the corridors of art school, in the hallways of Salford's social housing estates, in the working-class pubs of Greater Manchester — the frustration, the energy, the creative boredom — all of this finds in this evening a point of condensation.

The distinction between causality and catalysis is decisive here. Sumner and Hook do not need the Sex Pistols to want to make music; they need this concert to understand that it is possible, immediately possible, without technical training, without a contract, without commercial infrastructure. The lesson is not musical. It is political, in the most elementary sense: it says that the barriers are not where one believed them to be.

One must also note, as Clinton Heylin underlines, that Ian Curtis does not attend the first concert of 4 June. He will be present at the second Sex Pistols evening at the same venue, on 20 July 1976 — a different concert, in a different context, with an already fuller room⁸. This temporal difference in exposure to punk among the respective members contributes to Joy Division's particular dynamic: Sumner and Hook experience an initial revelation that Curtis approaches with a slight delay, and already a certain critical familiarity with the punk aesthetic from which the group precisely seeks to free itself. This is not an anecdote. It is a datum that subtly influences the internal relationships within the group, and the way in which each member positions himself with respect to the punk heritage that the group is precisely trying to transcend.

The concert of 4 June 1976 is therefore neither the sole cause of Joy Division, nor an event without importance. It is a detonator within a social and cultural landscape already charged, whose significance has been amplified, sharpened and sometimes distorted by decades of retrospective accounts. The historian must work with this double status: real event and active myth, without confusing them.
