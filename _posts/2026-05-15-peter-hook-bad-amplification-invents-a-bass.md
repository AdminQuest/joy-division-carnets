# Peter Hook: when bad amplification invents a bass sound

Peter Hook does not enter Joy Division as a predestined bass player. At the beginning, there is no clearly stated instrumental vocation, no method, no mastery. There is a simple, established fact: after the Mancunian punk shock, Bernard Sumner already has a guitar; Hook therefore takes the bass. The division of roles emerges from a minimal economy. One guitar, one bass, a group to bring into being. Everything else comes later.[^1]

Hook’s retrospective testimony gives this scene an almost comic sharpness. He borrows money from his mother, goes to Mazel in Manchester, and buys a Gibson EB-0 copy without really knowing what he is choosing. The instrument arrives in a black bag. Nothing in this episode yet resembles an aesthetic birth. It is a beginner’s purchase, made urgently, with the means available. Caution is nevertheless necessary: the memory is direct, but it is reconstructed after the fact, from the position of a musician who has become one of the most recognisable bass players in post-punk history.[^2]

The learning process follows the same logic. Hook learns by doing. He does not come from the technical tradition of bass playing, does not belong to a school, does not attempt to reproduce an established model properly. He discovers the instrument through trial, error and necessity. This ignorance of convention plays a real role, but it should not be fetishised. Punk mythology likes to turn incompetence into purity. The history is more interesting than that: Hook does not play « better » because he does not know how to play; he invents solutions because he does not yet possess the expected gestures.

The material constraint intensifies this process. According to Hook, the episode of the Celestion 18, bought cheaply from Mr Hubbard, his former art teacher connected with the Salford Jets, produces a paradoxical result. The low notes sound bad. Against Sumner’s amplifier, Hook cannot properly hear himself when he plays in the lower register. So he moves up the neck. He plays higher. What begins as a problem of audibility becomes a form. Ian Curtis, in Hook’s account, hears something there: Sumner’s guitar can occupy the chords, Hook’s bass can trace the upper lines, Stephen Morris can install his dry, nervous rhythms.[^3]

This point matters, but it has to be handled carefully. Hook readily identifies this episode as the origin of the Joy Division sound. It is a strong self-interpretation. It illuminates a scene. It does not exhaust causality.

For Joy Division’s sound is not born from a defective amplifier alone. It stabilises within a wider set of conditions: material poverty, rehearsal spaces, collective writing, Curtis’s structuring role, Morris’s precision, Sumner’s economy of playing, and later Martin Hannett’s studio work. Hook’s high-register bass is a central part of the system, not its total explanation. That is precisely what makes it interesting. It does not dominate by itself; it opens up space.

In « Transmission », « Disorder », « She’s Lost Control » or « Shadowplay », the bass does not merely support the structure. It moves forward. It draws a clear line through music that, without it, might sometimes thicken or close in on itself. It becomes an instrumental voice, almost a counter-melody. Critical interpretation can then speak of a reversal of function: the instrument usually placed underneath the song steps forward, not to shine, but to orient the listener’s ear.

Nor should Hook be isolated from his influences. In his own account, Jean-Jacques Burnel of the Stranglers matters for the sound; Paul Simonon of the Clash matters for the stance. The exact short formula attributed to Hook on this point still needs to be rechecked before direct quotation, but the idea is stable: his playing is built between sonic aggression and stage image.[^4] Here again, the signature does not come from a single source. It assembles. It digests. It bends references through a particular physical method: the bass worn very low, the line played high on the neck, an attack that can seem unstable, a tension between pose and effort.

Another testimony further complicates the story. Deborah Curtis reports that an internal episode of tension, linked to Bernard Sumner’s absence and to the idea of adding a second guitarist, helps push Hook’s bass forward in the group’s balance.[^5] The passage is valuable, but fragile. It does not prove that the centrality of the bass begins with this incident. It points instead to a moment of rebalancing. Joy Division is still searching for itself. The group gradually understands that its strength does not lie in adding another instrument, but in the exact tension between four functions.

The legend therefore retains a simple image: Hook plays high because his amplifier does not allow him to hear himself in the low register. The image is correct, but incomplete. The material fact exists. The retrospective testimony organises it. Critical interpretation gives it aesthetic significance. The myth, by contrast, would like to make one bad purchase the single key to Joy Division.

That temptation has to be resisted.

What emerges with Hook is not only a bass style. It is a way of turning defect into axis, amateurism into method, constraint into melodic line. Nothing is written in advance. Nothing leads mechanically from Mazel to *Unknown Pleasures*. Yet one thing gradually fixes itself: in this group, the bass does not stay in its place. It rises out of the floor. It moves into the cold light. It becomes the clear nerve of a black music.
